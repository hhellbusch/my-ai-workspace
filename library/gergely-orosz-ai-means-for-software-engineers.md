## About

Wide-ranging interview covering Orosz's engineering career (Skype → Uber → Pragmatic Engineer) and what AI means for the profession — emotionally, practically, and strategically. The most quotable section is on the novice/expert gap with AI and why domain expertise is the differentiator. ## Key themes

### AI grief — the loss of coding as a grit selector

> "It was hard to do it. Still is very hard to learn to be a good developer. And just the coding aspect — throw away AI — and most people will give up. They will not push through. So it was just a really good selector for grit, for smartness... and now AI comes in and everyone can do it."

The coding interview was a filter for persistence and cognitive ability. With AI writing code competently, that filter breaks. The feeling of devaluation after 10+ years of investment is real and legitimate to acknowledge.

### The expert/novice gap with AI — "fluency is not evidence"

Orosz asked three LLMs the same legal question; all three gave confident, well-reasoned answers pointing to outcome A. The lawyer later answered B — because the regulation is never interpreted how it's written. The AI had no access to judicial interpretation patterns.

> "As a novice, I would have absolutely believed ChatGPT. As an expert, you know what good looks like — you know when it's wrong."

Experts catch AI errors because they have a reference for "what good looks like." Novices don't. This is why AI accelerates experts and can mislead novices. It's also why the person deploying an AI system in a high-stakes context still needs domain expertise — not to generate the output, but to evaluate it.

### Product-minded engineers — the template for AI-era success

Engineers who succeed long-term (documented by Orosz since 2019):
- Understand business goals, not just technical requirements
- Proactively meet with product managers (one-on-one, unprompted)
- Bring trade-off suggestions to planning meetings ("we could take this out and ship in 3 weeks vs. 3 months")
- Can prioritize and say no to non-priority work

This template predates AI but is now the default expectation rather than the differentiator.

### Leap of abstraction — compiler history as the map

Grady Booch's framing: going from assembly to high-level languages was structurally identical to what's happening now. Old-timers who resisted compilers ("a compiler will never write as efficient code as we can") lost. Old-timers who embraced high-level languages *while* retaining their hardware understanding became the most capable engineers — they had both layers.

Same pattern now. The winners: engineers who embrace AI tools while retaining fundamental understanding. The danger: assuming AI removes the need to understand the underlying thing.

### Boris Cherny — radical adaptability as a model

Creator of Claude Code; uses it for 100% of code generation (most Anthropic engineers: 70–90%). Famous prior: broke both arms in a motorcycle accident → moved to functional programming to minimize keystrokes → became excellent at it. Pattern: no attachment to current tools, maximum attachment to problem-solving ability.

### Engineering career trends

- Teams shrinking: "two pizza" → "one pizza" (2–4 engineers)
- Engineering manager roles becoming scarce and less lucrative
- Traditional manager → senior manager → director path compressing to: tech lead + director
- AI-era managers must be able to "pick up a shovel" — do the work their teams do

### Career myths

- **Job hopping myth:** Works in hot markets, becomes a red flag at leadership levels. Long tenure shows impact AND proves you didn't just leave when your decisions had consequences.
- **"Just do great work" myth:** Correct but incomplete — at large companies, visibility is necessary. The best engineers make sure the right people know what they did.
- **Empty GitHub = not passionate myth:** Best engineers often leave at 6pm, have empty GitHub, and do 8 hours of focused quality work. OSS activity is a proxy that doesn't capture what matters.
