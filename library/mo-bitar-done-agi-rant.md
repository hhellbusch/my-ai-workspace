## About

A passionate rant after 12 hours of reading about AGI. Mo's conclusion: AGI is an undefined term, the specialization argument defeats the generality argument for coding, LLMs fail because of context not intelligence, and he refuses to extrapolate from the present. David Deutsch's *The Beginning of Infinity* is the trigger.

---

## The specialization argument

> "For things like coding, you don't want generality. You want the opposite. You want specialization. The mid-level engineer on your team who's been working in your codebase for 10 years writes better code than a PhD-level engineer who just joined."

AGI wouldn't help here. The problem isn't insufficient general intelligence — it's the inability to carry years of accumulated domain context.

> "The problem with LLMs today is they can't hold a damn thing in its tiny head. Not just the codebase itself, but the intangibles — the business context, the politics, the reason your designer will quit if you change the color of the button."

---

## The mechanism argument (David Deutsch)

> "LLMs are an elaborate trick. There is no theory in there. It's brute-forcing statistics to trick people for long durations."
> "You can polish a lie forever and it never becomes the truth. You can put a billion more parameters on the lie and it's a bigger lie now."

The Turing test's real question, per Mo: whether the human evaluating it is gullible enough to be fooled. The mechanism matters — statistics passing as intelligence isn't intelligence.

---

## The anti-extrapolation posture

> "From now on, I'm going to take the present state of LLM and treat that as the only fact that exists. I will behave as though there is no such thing as progress. There is only now."

Operationally: judge AI tools on what they do today, not on what they might do in 12–18 months. "Everything else is fear, confusion, uncertainty, doubt, and propaganda."
