## About

Mo Bitar on the economics of AI access, triggered by the Eric Schmidt graduation speech (where students booed every AI mention) and Peter Steinberger spending $1.3M on tokens in a month doing a science experiment. The central argument: AI is getting more expensive for the models that matter, not cheaper — and this is structurally intentional.

---

## The two pricing charts deception

> "There's been two pricing charts. One for the lower-end models — those prices have been going down or staying the same. But they made a new chart which is the model that you actually have to use if you want to do any meaningful work. The price of those tokens have been going way up."

Specific data points:
- GPT 5.4 → 5.5: token price doubled
- Anthropic Opus 4.7: same nominal price as 4.6, but new tokenizer produces 35% longer responses → effective ~35% price increase
- OpenRouter study: GPT 5.5 vs 5.4 actual cost was 49–92% higher

The Moore's Law analogy for token costs is wrong — it only holds for the tier of models that can't do meaningful agentic work.

## AI psychosis (Mitchell Hashimoto)

> "Companies are optimizing for fixing issues quicker rather than having less issues — because AI sucks at not creating issues. It's a bug machine. But it's really good at fixing issues very quickly."

Companies have a hypothesis: ship slightly more buggy code in 1/10th the time, fix issues after. Whether that's sustainable long-term is the open question.

## The access gap

Peter Steinberger (OpenClaw founder, now at OpenAI) spent $250k in 7 days and $1.3M in a month running a science experiment: "how would we build software if tokens didn't matter?"

Mo's rejoinder: tokens do matter. Massively. The experiment is only available to people at the frontier companies. The rocket ship Eric Schmidt described has three seats, already taken.

> "AI is here and you will not be able to afford it. It's not for you."

## The Eric Schmidt moment

University of Arizona commencement: students booed every time Schmidt said "AI." He kept saying it. He did not adjust. The crowd didn't stop booing. Mo's reading: the moment the cultural bubble around tech optimism became visible from inside.
