## Key Differences (summarized)

1. **Higher stances** — Okinawan stances are natural and practical; Japanese stances are deeper, built for leg strength and visual impact in large groups.

2. **"Why" over "How"** — Okinawan karate emphasizes *imi* (meaning) and bunkai (purpose of kata). Japanese karate focuses on execution detail (how to twist hips, adjust feet). Three reasons: meaning was lost in transmission, Japanese karate shifted toward character development, and Japanese biomechanics research runs deeper.

3. **No "Osu/Oss"** — Never used in Okinawa. Okinawans use "Hai!"

4. **Not a sport, a lifestyle** — Japanese karate practitioners changed kata and added techniques for scoring competition points. In Okinawa, karate is cultural identity. "A growing bamboo does not compete with the bamboo next to it."

5. **Chinkuchi over kime** — Japanese karate values *kime* (the decisive stop). Okinawan karate values *chinkuchi* — explosive transfer of full-body power into the opponent, like a shockwave.

6. **Kobudo weapons** — Almost every Okinawan dojo has weapons on the wall. "Karate and Kobudo is like brother and sister. Never separate." — Nakamoto Masahiro, 10th dan. Rarely practiced in Japanese karate.

7. **Old-school strength tools** — Makiwara, chi-ishi, ishi-sashi, nigiri-game, tou. Essential in Okinawa, rare in Japan (except makiwara). "A dojo without a makiwara is not a dojo."

8. **Tuidi techniques** — Okinawan karate prefers close range: grabbing, seizing, twisting, dislocating joints, choking, throwing, pressure points. Japanese karate adopted longer-range maai from sword arts. Original kata bunkai makes much more sense at close range.

9. **Individuals over mass training** — Okinawan dojos hold 10-15 people with personal sensei attention. Japanese karate was tailored for university groups of 50+. This is why Japanese movements are bigger and more simplified — they need to be seen by masses.

10. **Uchinaa-guchi** — Okinawan karate has its own language. Terms like chinkuchi, tuidi, muchimi, gamaku are not Japanese — they are ancient Okinawan language.

**Bonus — #11: Dojo atmosphere.** In Okinawa, nobody tries to hurt you or outwork you, no holier-than-thou sensei aura. "Everyone are there as friends, working together in the spirit of Karate."

**Jesse's conclusion:** "In the end, it's about doing what you love — whether it's 'Okinawan' or 'Japanese'."
