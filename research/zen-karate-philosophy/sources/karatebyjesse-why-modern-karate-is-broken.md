## Key Ideas

### The origin story
In 1921, Japan was looking for a domestic alternative to Western boxing to boost national spirit. Okinawan *toudi* ("Chinese Hand") was the answer. Funakoshi Gichin was "the chosen one" — educated, spoke good Japanese, had practiced since youth.

### The systematization
Toudi had to conform to Japanese martial arts culture:
- Name changed from "Chinese Hand" to "Empty Hand" (Karate-Do) in 1933 — Japan hated Chinese connections
- Belt system, kyu/dan ranks, uniforms introduced
- Ritualized acts, Japanese terminology added
- "Styles" invented — every sensei had to register a name with Dai Nippon Butokukai
- New instructor license "renshi" created — existing titles (shihan, kyoshi, hanshi) considered "too noble for common island people"
- Tournament rules, safety regulations added

### What broke
Toudi was a complete fighting art: strikes, kicks, grappling, throws, joint locks, ground techniques, pressure points, weapons. But Japan already had those things in their own martial arts. So karate's technical registry was decimated — "ruthlessly pigeonholed" into the 3K format: **kata, kihon, kumite**.

### Mabuni's admission
"The Karate that has been introduced to Tokyo is actually just a part of the whole. The fact that those who have learnt Karate feel it only consists of kicks and punches, and that throws and joint locks are only found in Judo or Ju-Jutsu, can only be put down to a lack of understanding." — Kenwa Mabuni

### The fix: bunkai
The original techniques are "hidden in plain sight" — embedded in kata. Bunkai (practical application analysis) is the key to recovering what was lost. "Understand kata to understand Karate. Your link to the past is your bridge to the future."
